Q & A

What are your tolerances?

Cutting is normally within 0.25mm.

Bending has many influences – we normally work on +/- 1mm. Critical bending tolerance needs to be communicated.

Key Bending Parameters

  • Minimum Bend Radius: This is the smallest possible radius for a bend without the material cracking or deforming undesirably.
    • Soft materials like aluminium and mild steel: minimum bend radius is often 1× the material thickness.
    • Harder materials like the other metals listed: a radius of ≥ 2× the material thickness is generally required to prevent cracking.
    • General rule: For materials between 2–6 mm thick, a radius of 1× thickness is often acceptable; for thicker materials, 2–3× is recommended.
  • Minimum Flange Length: This is the shortest possible length of material that can be successfully bent on a die.
    • General rule: Minimum flange length of 4× the material thickness or 12mm, whichever is greater.
  • Z-Bends: Maintain a specific minimum X-measurement (distance from the bend to the edge) to prevent tool collisions.
  • Feature Spacing: Features like holes or other bends need to be placed a sufficient distance from bend lines.
    • General rule: Keep features at least 4× the material thickness away from bend lines to avoid issues during manufacturing.
  • Curls: For more complex curls, the spacing from other features should be a minimum of the curl radius plus the sheet metal thickness.

Tolerances

  • Bend Angle Tolerance: ±1°
  • Length Tolerance: ±0.20mm (0.010 inches) per bend
  • ISO 2768: Widely recognized reference for general tolerances in precision sheet metal manufacturing (linear, angular, geometric).

Do you have tips for effective bending?

  • Avoid acute angles: Do not design for angles sharper than 130°.
  • Avoid extreme obtuse angles: Do not design for angles greater than 175°.
  • Be mindful of successive bends: Place bends as far apart as possible; successive bends can be difficult to fit on the die and complicate manufacturing.
  • Consider material hardness: The specific material and its yield strength are crucial factors for bend radii and feasibility.

What metals do you offer?

If you need something not in this list, please reach out to us.

Mild Steel

Mild steel (low-carbon steel) contains ~0.05%–0.25% carbon. Its low carbon content makes it ductile, malleable, and easy to work with—widely used in construction, manufacturing, and fabrication.

3CR12 Stainless Steel

3CR12 is a corrosion-resistant utility ferritic stainless steel developed in South Africa by Columbus Stainless. It bridges the gap between carbon/mild steel and more expensive austenitic grades (like 304 and 316), offering a balance of corrosion resistance, strength, and cost-effectiveness.

430 Stainless Steel

430 is a straight-chromium ferritic stainless steel (~16–18% chromium, little to no nickel). It’s widely used for its good corrosion resistance, formability, and affordability compared to austenitic grades like 304.

304 Stainless Steel

The most widely used stainless grade (austenitic). Excellent corrosion resistance, durability, and formability; often called the “workhorse” of stainless steels.

316 Stainless Steel

A premium austenitic stainless steel with exceptional corrosion resistance, especially in harsh and chloride-rich environments (often called “marine grade stainless”). Performs better than 304 in seawater and de-icing salts.

Aluminium 1050

Commercially pure aluminium (≥99.5%). Excellent corrosion resistance, high ductility, and very good thermal/electrical conductivity. Non-heat-treatable; strength is via cold working (work hardening).

How do I design a part?

  • Refer to the How it works section for guiding comments.
  • Have an idea of the shape and size. If it must fit into a space or assembly, measure carefully.
  • If you can’t produce a CAD model, send a picture or drawing with critical dimensions and intended material thickness.
  • If you need help to get to a design that solves your problem, please get in touch.
  • If you like to iterate yourself, consider learning CAD. There are free and paid packages (online and installable) with different workflows.
  • We prefer online tools for easy collaboration and lower hardware requirements.
  • Choose a tool with good online support and community forums.

What are the different surface finishes?

Refer to the Materials available page for which finishes are available for which metals.

Hot rolled

  • Surface finish: Rougher, with scale (oxide layer).
  • Dimensions: Less precise tolerances (can shrink while cooling).
  • Strength: Lower than cold rolled, but more ductile.
  • Cost: Cheaper due to less processing.

Cold rolled

  • Surface finish: Smooth, clean, often shiny.
  • Dimensions: Very precise thickness and shape control.
  • Strength: Higher tensile strength and hardness (work hardening).
  • Cost: More expensive due to extra processing.

Galvanised

  • Excellent corrosion resistance due to a zinc coating (protective and sacrificial).
  • Durable, cost-effective, low-maintenance, long lifespan.
  • Good strength, weldability, formability, and recyclability.

2D (cold rolling, annealing, pickling)

  • Appearance: Silvery-white, dull matte, non-reflective.
  • Smoothness: Smoother than No.1, less smooth than 2B.
  • Texture: Non-directional, uniform dull surface.
  • Corrosion resistance: Enhanced by chemical pickling.

No.1 (hot rolled, annealed, pickled)

  • Appearance: Rough, dull, off-white, non-uniform.
  • Cost-effective: Less processing.
  • Mechanical properties: High strength and durability.
  • Use: Ideal for heavy-duty, high-temperature applications.

BA (bright annealed in oxygen-free atmosphere)

  • Appearance: Smooth, reflective, bright—near mirror-like (≈70–90% reflectivity).
  • Hygiene: Non-porous; resists bacterial growth (great for sanitary use).
  • Corrosion resistance: Enhanced chromium oxide layer.

2B (cold-rolled, annealed, light cold pass)

  • Appearance: Smooth, bright but not fully mirrored; hazy, semi-reflective.
  • Pattern: Slight directional pattern from rolling.
  • Corrosion resistance: Enhanced, economical for functional use.

2B PVC (2B with protective film)

  • Supplied with removable protective PVC film to prevent scratches during handling and transport.

PVC BA (BA with protective film)

  • Supplied with removable protective PVC film to prevent scratches during handling and transport.

No.4 PVC (fine abrasive belts with protective film)

  • Appearance: Satin-like, non-reflective, uniform brushed look with short parallel grain.
  • Durability: Durable and often repairable with light abrasive pads; low-maintenance.
